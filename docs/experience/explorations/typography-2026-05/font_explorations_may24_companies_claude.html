What if BridgeCircle borrowed a type system, not a font?

The earlier files looked at fonts on their own. This one looks at type as a system — serif + sans + mono treated as one designed family, the way a few well-known companies handle it. Four examples, each rendered on the actual BridgeCircle UI moments. Where the company's fonts are paid, I've shown the closest free approximation and noted what's lost.

How to read the four options against BridgeCircle's brand

System 1 · Recommended

IBM  ·  IBM Plex Serif + Sans + Mono

Commissioned by IBM in 2017, designed by Mike Abbink with Bold Monday. Released under the SIL Open Font License — IBM's gift to the design community. Now used by IBM, Wikipedia, MIT Media Lab, and many universities and civic institutions. The three families (Plex Sans, Plex Serif, Plex Mono) were drawn together — they share proportions, x-height, and spacing so they read as one voice.

IBM treats type as part of identity, not decoration. Plex was drawn to feel "engineered yet humanist" — corporate without coldness. Mike Abbink famously said "the goal was a face that fits a software engineer and a research scientist equally." It now spans over 130 languages, including high-quality Korean (Hangul) — meaningful for the Chadwick International campus in Songdo.
BridgeCircle is closer to MIT or Wikipedia (verified-network, institutional trust) than to a tech-startup product. Plex Serif on the wordmark + headlines, Plex Sans for UI labels, Plex Mono for the existing tracked uppercase moments — replaces three current fonts (Fraunces, Inter, JetBrains Mono) with one designed system. The "Civic Editorial" voice resolves cleanly into "Plex Editorial."
Free on Google Fonts. Production-ready. All three families.
System 2

The New Yorker  ·  Caslon + Neutraface

The New Yorker has used variants of Caslon (originally William Caslon, 1722) for over 100 years — currently Adobe Caslon Pro for body and Caslon Pro Italic for the unmistakable pull-quotes and "Goings On" copy. Pairs with Neutraface 2 (House Industries) for sans deco accents and NYer-style headlines. Other Caslon users: most Ivy League print, much of the U.S. legal-academic world, and almost every prep-school alumni magazine.

Below is rendered in Libre Caslon Text + Public Sans, the closest free approximation. (True Adobe Caslon Pro would have more weights and a finer italic, but the silhouette is the same.)

The New Yorker uses type to assert continuity. Caslon was the default of the American Revolution (the Declaration of Independence is set in it). Reading something set in Caslon signals "this institution intends to be around in 50 years." That's the exact signal a verified alumni network is selling.
If Chadwick's brand leans on "the school's institutional weight" — founded 1935, alumni magazine voice, prep-school heritage — this is the strongest editorial choice in any of the three explorations. The pull-quote on the profile card ("Cardiologist interested in helping pre-med students.") is exactly where Caslon italic earns its keep.
Adobe Caslon Pro requires Adobe Fonts subscription (Creative Cloud). For self-hosted free use, Libre Caslon Text on Google Fonts is the closest substitute — only 400/700 weights, italic at 400. Acceptable for production if you're willing to live without semibold; tight if you need a real weight ladder. Neutraface 2 is paid (House Industries); Public Sans is the cleanest free substitute.
System 3

GitHub  ·  Mona Sans + Hubot Sans

GitHub commissioned Mona Sans (Degarism Studio, 2022) and Hubot Sans (2023) as their identity system. Both are variable fonts — one file covers weight + width axes — and both are SIL-licensed. Used across github.com, the GitHub Universe brand, and GitHub's product documentation. Mona is the workhorse; Hubot is the "weirder cousin" reserved for headlines and the wordmark. Together they're deliberately not Inter — GitHub explicitly wanted a face that wasn't generic sans-tech.

GitHub made type a craft moment. Hubot's wider, slightly square letterforms feel engineered without being soulless — they read as "we drew our own thing because it mattered to us." That signal — craft applied to the boring parts — is exactly what GitHub wants to project to developers. It's the antithesis of pulling Inter from a CDN.
Notice the headline above — no serif at all. This is the most modern of the four options. If BridgeCircle wants to lean away from "editorial heritage" and toward "considered modern tooling," this is the move. The lack of a serif removes one of the brand's current characters (the Fraunces pull-quote), which may or may not be what you want.
Free on Google Fonts. Both variable. Best loaded with the opsz and wdth axes via next/font/google. No serif means .bc-pull-quote needs to be rebuilt as a sans treatment (italic + heavier rule) — small refactor.
System 4

Stripe  ·  Söhne + Tiempos

Stripe set the modern "premium fintech" type standard. Their stack: Söhne (Klim Type Foundry, 2019) for sans, occasionally paired with Tiempos Headline (also Klim) for editorial pages. Both designed by Kris Sowersby in New Zealand. Now imitated by half of Series A SaaS — but used directly only by companies with a Klim license, which is part of the signal. Other Klim users: Substack, Medium Pro, Linear for marketing, OpenAI for some surfaces.

Below is rendered with Inter + Vollkorn as the closest free approximation. The fidelity to Söhne is poor (Inter is rounder and chunkier than Söhne); Vollkorn approximates Tiempos's warmth but not its high contrast.

Stripe's typography reads as "calm authority." Söhne is restrained — no quirks, no warmth pretending to be charm — and Tiempos italic is the most photographed italic in tech. The brand's confidence is partly because Stripe paid for fonts other people don't have, and that exclusivity translates into "the brand is taking itself seriously."
BridgeCircle's brand voice (Civic Editorial, considered) is more aligned with Stripe than with GitHub or IBM in tone. But the cost of the actual Klim license for a small alumni-network product is hard to justify; and the free approximation doesn't capture what makes it special. Listed here for inspiration, not as a practical pick.
Söhne + Tiempos: paid, licensed per-domain from Klim Type Foundry, ~$1,800 for a small business pack. If the brand justifies it later, this is the upgrade path. For now: pick a free system, ship, revisit if revenue arrives.

Recommendation: System 1 — IBM Plex (Serif + Sans + Mono)

Of the four, only Plex is both free and designed as a system. It replaces all three of BridgeCircle's current fonts (Fraunces, Inter, JetBrains Mono) with one designed family — one voice across wordmark, headline, body, label, mono, and code. Multi-script coverage matters for Chadwick International (Songdo). And the company association (IBM, MIT, Wikipedia) puts the brand in institutional company without the "AI template" feeling.

If the brand decides it wants to lean harder into prep-school heritage, layer Libre Caslon Text on top of Plex for the wordmark and pull-quotes only — two-family system with Plex as the workhorse. Best of both signals.

// app_claude/src/app/layout.tsx — adopt IBM Plex as a full system. // Three current fonts (Fraunces, Inter, JetBrains Mono) become one family. import { IBM_Plex_Sans, IBM_Plex_Serif, IBM_Plex_Mono, Inter_Tight } from 'next/font/google' const sans = IBM_Plex_Sans({ variable: '--font-sans', subsets: ['latin'], weight: ['400', '500', '600', '700'], }) const serif = IBM_Plex_Serif({ variable: '--font-serif', subsets: ['latin'], weight: ['400', '500', '600', '700'], style: ['normal', 'italic'], }) const mono = IBM_Plex_Mono({ variable: '--font-mono', subsets: ['latin'], weight: ['400', '500'], }) // --font-display can keep Inter_Tight or also point to Plex Sans 700. // The .bc-fraunces helper class rename to .bc-display is the only cleanup.
