Type that doesn't sound like every other AI-generated SaaS.

The previous exploration covered safe, well-paired choices (Newsreader, Source Serif, Spectral). They're good — but they're showing up everywhere AI tools recommend fonts. This file is for the case where you want the page to look like someone with taste picked it, not a model that suggested the same five families it suggests to every startup.

Fonts I'd avoid because every AI template is using them

These are competent fonts. The problem isn't quality — it's that they've collapsed into a visual vocabulary you've seen on every YC landing page, every Vercel template, every Cursor demo. Picking one signals "default" rather than "considered."

  • Sans defaults: Inter, Geist, DM Sans, Plus Jakarta Sans, Manrope, Outfit, Cabinet Grotesk, Satoshi
  • Display serifs: Fraunces (current), Playfair Display, DM Serif Display, Cormorant Garamond, Instrument Serif
  • Borderline (mainstream but not slop yet): Newsreader, Source Serif, IBM Plex Sans (use these and you're still in good company, but they're trending toward the list above)

The four pairs below are deliberately off this map. All are free on Google Fonts.

Eczar was designed at the Indian Type Foundry by Vaibhav Singh as a scholarly serif for both Devanagari and Latin. It almost never appears in tech-startup defaults because most template authors don't go past Google's "popular" tab. Public Sans is the US Web Design System's workhorse — used by Federal agency sites, almost never by SaaS. High x-height (very readable at small UI sizes), generous spacing, and an unusually considered italic. Public Sans pairs naturally with serifs because it was drawn with institutional contexts in mind. Together they read as scholarly + civic, which is closer to "private school alumni network" than anything on the slop list. Eczar's letterforms are a little narrower than Fraunces, so long names can wrap sooner. Public Sans is less geometrically tidy than Inter at small sizes — the difference is small but noticeable in dense admin tables.

Literata was commissioned for Google Play Books by Type Together — it's purpose-built for sustained on-screen reading and has an optical-size axis that few free serifs have. Despite being on Google Fonts since 2018, it's almost never the suggestion because the obvious editorial serif slot is taken by Playfair/Fraunces in the template-author mind. Italics are exceptional — closer to a Klim or Commercial Type italic than anything else free. The italic pull-quote actually looks like an editorial pull-quote, not a calligraphy sample. Optical size means the wordmark can be set at opsz 36 while captions get the right adjustments at smaller sizes. Inter staying means zero disruption to the existing label rhythm. Inter is on the slop list. If you adopt Literata, consider rotating Inter out within 6 months to dodge the "Vercel template" feeling — see Pair 4 for the natural replacement.

Vollkorn means "wholegrain" in German — Althausen drew it as an everyday body serif, not a display face. It's been in Google Fonts since 2005 but you won't see it in AI templates because templates reach for showier serifs. Commissioner was drawn for institutional/civic communication; it's variable, has wide weight range, and reads warmer than IBM Plex Sans without falling into Inter-default territory. This is the most "warm institution" of the pairings — closer to how a thoughtful university or a Scandinavian public-service site would read. Both fonts have excellent diacritics, which matters for the Chadwick International (Songdo) member base. Vollkorn at small sizes can feel slightly bookish where you wanted brisk. The pair leans more "civic warm" than "private prep school" — depends which side of Chadwick's voice you want to lead with.

Faustina is from a small Argentinian foundry (Omnibus Type, by Alfonso García Martínez) and is almost invisible in US tech — most template authors don't browse past the English-language foundries. IBM Plex Sans is on the borderline of mainstream but has more institutional character than Inter; pairing it with a non-American serif keeps the combination distinctive. Faustina has slightly Latin-American editorial warmth (think the magazine work of Eduardo Manso) — confident headlines without the formality of Caslon. Plex Sans holds together for UI labels and admin tables. Plex Sans is one tier closer to the slop list than Public Sans or Commissioner. If you go this route, consider whether Plex's IBM associations are a feature (institutional credibility) or a tax (corporate-tech feel).

Recommendation: Pair 1 — Eczar + Public Sans

It's the strongest "considered" signal in the set. Eczar is genuinely uncommon in tech but is high quality and screen-tested; Public Sans is the most credible non-Inter sans for a network that wants to read as institutional. Both have generous x-heights, both age well, both are free. If you want a single-step move that puts BridgeCircle in visual company with the US Web Design System and academic publishing — and notably not with every Vercel template — this is the pick.

If Eczar reads too academic for the prep-school audience, Pair 3 (Vollkorn + Commissioner) is the warmer "civic + considered" alternative.
