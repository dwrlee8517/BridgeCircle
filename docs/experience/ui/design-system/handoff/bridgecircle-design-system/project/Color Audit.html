Brand color audit · v1

Should the palette have more spectrum?

A short answer: not really — but two existing colors are doing the same job. The system is intentionally restrained, and that restraint is working. The actual problem is overlap between Amber and Ochre, not absence of hue.

01 / Spectrum coverage

Where the palette sits on the hue wheel.

The 6 chromatic colors plotted by hue (angle) and chroma (distance from center). Blue anchors the brand on the cool side; the warm side carries four accents plus the Amber CTA. There is no green-blue, teal, violet, or magenta in the system.

This isn't a flaw — restrained palettes win editorial pages — but the warm cluster is crowded.

Reading the chart

Distance from center = how saturated the color is. Angle = its hue. Two dots near each other = visually similar at small sizes.

Amber (70°) and Ochre (56°) are 14° apart.

That's inside the perceptual confusion threshold for small UI elements (dots, badges, 12px text). A user can't reliably tell a "highest-stakes CTA" from a "warning / paused state" by color alone.

Rust and Plum are both warm reds.

Rust (34°, c=0.16) is doing destructive. Plum (16°, c=0.10) is doing taxonomy. They occupy nearby hues with different chromas — only the chroma carries the meaning, which is a fragile signal.

Cool side is clean.

Electric Sky and Sage are the only cool colors, ~110° apart. No overlap risk; Sage is the only green and unambiguously means success.

Empty quadrants: cyan, violet, magenta.

This is fine. Civic Editorial doesn't need them, and adding any would dilute "color = meaning." Keep them out.

02 / The overlap, at the size users see it

At 8 px, Amber and Ochre read as one color.

Status dots, progress bars, and badge pills are the sizes where the system relies most on color to carry meaning. Below, the two warm colors next to each other at their actual UI scale.

The fix isn't a wider palette — it's pushing Ochre further from Amber, or collapsing one of them.

04 / Three paths forward

Pick by appetite for change.

Listed cheapest to most invasive. The recommended option is the middle one — it fixes the real problem (Amber/Ochre collision) without diluting the editorial restraint.

Option A · hold

Keep the palette exactly as-is.

Accept the Amber/Ochre overlap as a known limitation. Disambiguate with iconography, position, and copy instead of color.

  • Zero token migration cost.
  • Editorial restraint stays absolute.
  • Status dots and tint pills remain ambiguous at 8–12 px.
  • Future "I need another color" requests will keep arriving.
Option B · recommended

Re-tune Ochre. Replace with a deeper amber.

Pull Ochre to #a16207 — same hue family, but ~10 points darker. Now it can't be confused with Amber CTA in a dot or pill. No new hues introduced.

  • Resolves the only real collision in the system.
  • "Warning" reads darker and more serious — closer to the editorial register.
  • Tints still work; one token swap.
  • Existing screenshots and email templates will need a regen.
Option C · expand

Add one cool neutral for info / AI.

Introduce a single desaturated slate #5b6373 for AI / match / informational signals — frees Primary to mean "interactive" only.

  • Disentangles "AI signal" from "primary action."
  • Slate is restrained — doesn't compete with editorial tone.
  • Real cost: new tints, badge variant, dark-mode pair.
  • Pressure to keep expanding ("we now have 7, why not 8") — needs strict governance.
Verdict

You don't need more hue. You need to space out the warm ones.

The palette already does what Civic Editorial asks of it: blue carries interaction, amber carries the single decisive moment, sage carries trust, midnight carries gravity. Adding a violet or a teal would soften that, not strengthen it.

The genuine fault line is the warm cluster between 16° and 70°. Amber and Ochre were specified at almost the same hue; the meanings they carry (CTA vs warning) are too close for users to discriminate at the sizes the UI uses them. Fix that, and the system is done.
